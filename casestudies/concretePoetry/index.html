Concrete Poetry is a form of linguistic art where the visual appearance of words and letters is as crucial as their meaning. The layout, typography, and spatial arrangement of text play a key role in conveying the poem's essence, often creating a fusion of visual and verbal elements.

ORIGINS        of                              concrete
poetry

In his 2008 introduction to Ohio State University’s visual poetry collection, Sackner distinguishes between concrete and visual poetry. Concrete poems rely solely on letters and words to create visual images, whereas visual poems integrate both images and text. 

A Calligram, for instance, arranges words in a specific shape related to the text's meaning. In contrast, a Concrete Poem emphasises the physical layout of words and letters, focusing on their placement to create meaning rather than shaping them into a recognisable form. Concrete Poets experiment with spatial relationships, using repetition, fragmentation, and unconventional spacing to generate a visual-verbal interaction.

CHALLENGING 
literary                       modernism

As a late phase of Literary Modernism, Concrete Poetry broke away from the expansive, complex linguistic structures favoured by earlier modernist writers. Instead, it reduced the text to a bare minimum, often using only a few words carefully placed to encapsulate profound ideas or emotions. 

This stark reduction marked a shift in literary experimentation, challenging traditional notions of poetic form. Concrete Poetry holds a unique position among avant-garde literary movements today. Unlike many other experimental forms, it enjoys more popularity outside of academic circles, appealing to a broader audience through its striking visual presentation.

HOW      is  a 
   calligram                                       different                      from a                                        concrete   poem?  

Concrete Poetry originated in the 1950s, influenced by Concrete Art in Northern Europe and Latin America. It sought to bring the clarity and simplicity of visual art to written language. Many early Concrete Poets were closely tied to Concrete Artists and often borrowed basic visual designs such as grids, spirals, and columns - directly from art pieces to arrange their text. 

This practice of simplifying complex ideas into minimalist forms reflected the modernist ideas seen in the International Style, known as the "architecture of the machine age," which 
embodied the crystallisation of modernism 
in building design.

Augusto de Campos 

Brazilian poet Augusto de Campos played
 a crucial role in developing and popularising Concrete Poetry, particularly in Latin America. He believed that words should be more than just carriers of meaning; they should also be visual elements that interact with the reader. Through his website and numerous publications, Campos continues to explore new ways to combine visual effects with linguistic content, transforming the text into an integral part of the poem’s visual experience.

Augusto de Campos' "Olho por Olho" (1964) is 
a significant example of Concrete Poetry that challenges both visual perception and reading. The poem creates a multidimensional experience by blending images and text, playing with illusions and offering multiple interpretations. It shifts between self-reference, metalanguage, and critical insight, encouraging readers to engage with its complex structure. The title, derived from 
a biblical phrase meaning "eye for eye," 
hints at a moral or vengeful theme, symbolised by a triangular traffic sign. As the reader approaches the poem, this sign 
appears to expand, adding a visual layer to its deeper message.

Décio Pignatari 

Décio Pignatari was a pioneering Brazilian poet, writer, and theorist, best known for his role in the development of Concrete Poetry. Alongside Augusto and Haroldo de Campos, he 
co-founded the Noigandres group, which was central to the concretist movement in Brazil. Pignatari's work is characterized by its sharp social and political commentary, often critiquing consumerism, advertising, and cultural imperialism. 

His poem Beba Coca-Cola (1957) is a sharp critique of advertising culture. By isolating parts of the Coca-Cola slogan and playing with their meanings—such as "cola" (glue) and "coca" (a stimulant)—Pignatari exposes the insidious power of commercial slogans. The poem’s use of red and white, the colours of Coca-Cola, reinforces its satirical tone, transforming a familiar brand into a critique of addiction and cultural degradation. 

Eugen Gomringer 

Eugen Gomringer, do you think, 2005. Silkscreen on canvas, 120 x 95 cm. Courtesy of the artist.

Eugen Gomringer one of the pioneers of Concrete Poetry, emphasises the visual arrangement of text. His work focuses on minimalist forms that challenge traditional poetry by emphasising the physicality of words, turning them into visual objects that create meaning through their placement on the page. Gomringer's approach set the groundwork for Concrete Poetry as a distinct art form, blending linguistic and visual elements.

Pierre Garnier

Poem by Pierre Garnier: "Pik bou," from the collection *Ozieux* (1966).

Pierre Garnier contributed to Concrete Poetry by expanding the spatial possibilities of text. His work often arranged letters and words in abstract forms, creating visual compositions that invite the reader to interpret meaning not only through language but also through the physical arrangement on the page. Garnier’s spatial poems push the boundaries of traditional verse, making the visual layout as important as the written content.

Carl Andre 

Cover: Carl Andre, intimate (detail), 1963, typewriter ink on paper, rubber cemented to backing page

Carl Andre, though better known as a minimalist sculptor, has also contributed to the world of Concrete Poetry. His works strip down both language and form to their bare essentials, focusing on the physical layout of text. Andre's approach mirrors his minimalist art, reducing complex ideas to simple, structured forms that invite the viewer to engage with language as a visual medium.
